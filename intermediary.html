INTERMEDIARY

LET'S BEGIN

The intermediary part of trap production is a time when the music begins to gain body and personality. At this stage, the producer takes the initial idea, usually consisting of a basic melody and a simple rhythm, and develops it into a richer, more complex structure. The beats, which are the heart of trap, become detailed with variations in hi-hats, additional layers of synthesizers and sound textures that bring depth to the sound.

ELEMENTS

  • Variations on hi-hats: Add quick sequences of notes (rolls) at specific times.
  • Snares: Let's enrich the battery even more
  • Kicks: Sometimes the 808 is combined with other bass sounds or kicks to add more punch or texture. This helps differentiate the 808's sound from other beats.
  • Open-hats: Open hi-hats, great for complementing the drums

HI-HATS

The intermediary part of hi-hats in trap production is where they gain complexity and variation, playing a crucial role in creating the groove and energy of the track. After establishing a basic pattern, the producer begins to introduce details and dynamism, making the hi-hats more engaging and less repetitive.

Rolls on hi-hats are a fundamental technique in trap production to create rhythmic variations and add movement to the beat. They are basically quick sequences of notes that can be customized in various ways, as shown in the image to the side.

SNARE

The snare is one of the most important elements in a trap beat, being responsible for setting the rhythm and adding impact to the track. It is generally used on the backbeat, appearing on beats 2 and 4 of the bar, creating the basis of the groove along with the kick and hi-hats. In trap, the snare is also a space for experimentation, going beyond the traditional "dry snap" to include different textures, layers and styles.

Trap snares often have a crisp, sharp, crackling sound, but can be enriched with layers of claps, rimshots, or even metallic textures.

KICK

Mixing the kick with the 808 is a fundamental part of trap production, as both occupy the same low frequency range and need to coexist harmoniously so that the beat has impact without sounding confused or cluttered. A good mix between these two elements ensures that the punch of the kick and the deep sustain of the 808 complement each other, creating a clean and powerful sound.

Shorter kicks with a strong attack (well-defined transient) tend to work better with 808s that have a longer decay, while lower kicks can mix well with drier and shorter 808s.

OPEN-HAT

Open hats (or open hi-hats) play an important role in trap production, serving as rhythmic elements that help fill the spaces between kicks and snares, as well as adding texture and energy to the beat. Although they are used less frequently than closed hi-hats, open hats have a significant impact on the dynamics and groove of the song.

They can be used to highlight the "downbeat" (first beat of the measure) or to create smooth transitions between different sections.

They help prevent the beat from sounding too tight or repetitive, creating a contrast with closed hi-hats, which tend to be faster and denser.
