ADVANCED

LET'S BEGIN

Advanced trap drum production involves a series of creative techniques and approaches to transform a simple beat into something complex and dynamic that has impact and flow. Although the basis of trap production is relatively simple, advanced producers explore layers, textures, and effects that make the rhythm more interesting and engaging. Here are some of the most common practices in advanced trap drum production:

ELEMENTS

  • Life in the hi-hats: Let's learn how and why to change hi-hat velocity.
  • 808: In some styles, the 808 is used together with the slide
  • Automation: Automation is a powerful tool for creating variations in volume, pitch, and effects throughout the song.
  • Use of effects: Effects can be applied to the mix of each element of the beat.

HI-HATS

The velocity of the hi-hat (or any other instrument in a beat) is a crucial parameter in trap production, as it defines the intensity with which the notes are played, directly impacting the dynamics and "feeling" of the rhythm. By adjusting the velocity, you can control the volume, perceived energy, and even the sound character of the hi-hats, making the beat more natural and expressive. Let's better understand how velocity affects the hi-hat and how to use it creatively in trap production.

When you set the velocity of a hi-hat to a high value, the sound will be stronger and clearer, with a sharper attack. This can be useful when you want an aggressive, punchy hi-hat in the mix.

808

In the context of the 808, slide is when the bass note (usually the 808) changes from one frequency to another, but smoothly and continuously, without an abrupt attack. Instead of playing one note and then going directly to the next, the note slides or gradually transitions between them. This creates a glissando effect (a seamless transition between notes).

This effect is commonly used in melodic sequences and bass lines to give a sense of movement and flow to the music, generating a unique vibe that has become one of the characteristics of modern trap.

AUTOMATION

Automation in music production, especially in trap, is an essential technique for giving dynamics, variation and expression to different elements of music, such as effects, volume, panning, frequencies and even the instrumentation itself. By applying automation, you can create transitions, modulations and effects that would be difficult to achieve with just static programming of notes or sounds.

Volume automation is one of the most basic and most used forms of automation. It allows you to increase or decrease the volume of a sound (or an entire track) during music, without having to manually adjust the levels.

EFFECTS

The use of effects is an essential part of trap production and many other musical genres. They add depth, texture and dynamics to music, creating unique atmospheres and helping to shape a track's sonic identity. In trap, effects are often used in a creative and exaggerated way to emphasize certain moments, such as build-ups, drops, breakdowns and transitions.

One of the most used effects in trap is reverb, which adds a sense of spatiality and ambiance to the sound. It simulates the way sound behaves in different types of spaces, such as a small room or a large cathedral. In trap music, reverb is often used to create a more open and ethereal atmosphere, especially in moments of introspection or in melodies and vocals. This effect can be subtle, adding a slight depth, or exaggerated, creating a feeling of distance and an immersive environment.
